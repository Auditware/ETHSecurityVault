## Description

## Anticapture: catching attacks before they drain your treasury

In March 2024 we found a way to drain about $150M from the ENS treasury ([disclosure](https://paragraph.com/@blockful/a-hidden-threat-to-ens-uncovering-and-solving-a-major-governanc)). Nothing in the code was broken. The problem sat one layer above it: a two-block gap between proposal creation and the vote snapshot, a timelock that couldn't cancel anything, and a handful of wallets large enough that someone could plausibly buy or borrow their way to quorum. We told ENS quietly, helped them design a 4-of-8 Security Council with veto power ([forum thread](https://discuss.ens.domains/t/temp-check-governance-security-compensating-blockful-for-preventing-a-potential-attack-on-the-ens-dao/19710)), and only published the write-up once the fix was in. No funds moved. That's what governance security looks like when it works.

  


  


Anticapture is the platform we built around that kind of work. It reads governance activity onchain (delegations, voting power, proposal flow, treasury exposure) and turns it into something a DAO can actually act on, ideally before anything goes wrong.

  


  


Today we cover **11 DAOs** , against **16 security indicators** , sorted into **3 maturity stages** , with a framework grounded in **30+ historical attacks** ([framework](https://blockful.gitbook.io/anticapture/anticapture/framework)).

  


## The layer no one is auditing

Smart contract audits look at code. Anticapture looks at the layer above: how decisions get made, who can make them, and what it would cost an attacker to take over. That layer is where the largest DAO losses have happened. Beanstalk lost $182M to a governance flash-loan in April 2022. Mango Markets lost $114M later that year in an oracle attack the attacker then ratified through a vote. Tornado Cash, Build Finance, Audius and others followed. Across documented incidents, governance and governance-adjacent attacks have cost the ecosystem well over $300M ([a16z research](https://a16zcrypto.com/posts/article/dao-governance-attacks-and-how-to-avoid-them/)).

  


  


None of these were code bugs, which is exactly why audits don't catch them. They're economic, behavioral, and structural. Almost no one is monitoring that surface continuously. We are.

  


## What we do

Free and public on [anticapture.com](https://anticapture.com):

  


  * a dashboard with risk stages for every DAO we cover
  * real-time alerts on proposals, power shifts, and delegation changes
  * open research on how governance attacks actually unfold
  * the Anticapture Framework itself, which any DAO can use to grade itself



  


Commissioned work for DAOs that want to go deeper:

  


  * calldata review, where we check that what a proposal says it does is what it actually does onchain
  * full governance audits covering delegation concentration, timelock behavior, quorum economics, and upgrade paths
  * security upgrades and data monitoring as a service provider, working based on Anticapture indicators to reach a higher security stage 



  


## Who we work with

We're an active delegate, grantee, or service provider with ENS, Uniswap, Arbitrum, Optimism, Scroll, and Superfluid ([contributions](https://blockful.io/contributions)). Our review of Shutter surfaced a path where roughly $100K worth of governance tokens could capture a treasury worth more than $3M ([article](https://paragraph.com/@blockful/shutter-attack-prevention)). Compound, Nouns, Gitcoin, Obol and several other DAOs are continuously monitored on the dashboard.

  


  


## Why this round, and why now

Treasuries worth millions, guarding protocols of billions, are being protected by voting systems that are hardly being checked. Delegation keeps concentrating. Quorum economics shift faster than most DAO ops teams can track. The Ethereum Security QF round exists because security work done before an exploit is the cheapest security work there is, and governance is the next obvious place for that work to land.

  


  


  


Our application fits several of the round's stated categories at once: security research and auditing, threat intelligence and on-chain investigation, security tooling and infrastructure, and security standards. It also fills a slot the round doesn't explicitly name yet, which is governance security itself.
