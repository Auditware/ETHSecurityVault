## Description

# Protecting Users with Protected Self Custody.

Ethereum wallets have always been the target. Phishing, malware, malicious dApps, compromised key infrastructure and the attack surface has grown every year. What's changed is who, and what, is holding the keys.

  


There are ~250,000 AI agents active on-chain at the date of this writing, and that number is growing fast. By the end of 2026, most on-chain transactions are expected to be agent-driven. Agents are signing transactions, moving capital, and managing portfolios without any human directly in the loop.

  


The problem is **not** that agents are autonomous. _This is a good thing._ The real problem is that there is no native proof that a human — sometimes with certain attributes — ever authorized any of it. A signature is a signature, and anyone with access to a private key can generate it. So how do we tell agents from humans, and more specifically which agents are acting dutifully on behalf of true human authority?

  


 We have built two key primitives to solve this problem:

 1\. Secure Secrets Management — keep humans in control of the loop by securing their keys (waap.xyz)

 2\. Proof of Humanity — prove the human in the loop is likely human (passport.xyz)

  


 Wallet as a Protocol (waap.xyz) enforces what agents can do. Passport is the identity layer that proves who authorized them to do it. This maps directly to what ERC-8004 and the Ethereum Foundation's dAI team are building toward: an identity and validation standard for trustless agents that traces back to real humans.

  


  


## WaaP: Who Needs Keys when you have Collaborative Signing?

[https://docs.waap.xyz](https://docs.waap.xyz/for-agents)

  


Wallet as a Protocol (WaaP) is wallet infrastructure for autonomous agents, built on 2PC (Two-Party Computation) cryptography to shore up an aggressive security model:

  


  1. Don't trust the device (malware exists)
  2. Don't trust the user (makes errors at 3am)
  3. Don't trust the app embedding the wallet (can be malicious)
  4. Don't trust the smart contract (could be zero day r.i.p.)
  5. Don't trust the key infra (bug, collusion)
  6. And absolutely don't trust the agent with the private key.



  


How do we do this? _The private key in practice doesn't exist._ Instead it is split into two shares, the Sovereign Share (k₁) and the Security Share (k₂). The full key k = k₁ + k₂ is never reconstructed anywhere. Instead, when a transaction needs signing, both parties run an interactive protocol to jointly compute a valid ECDSA signature. Each party contributes their share to produce a signature (s) from a message (m): s = r⁻¹(H(m) + x·Rₓ) mod n, where x is the private key, r is the nonce, and n is the curve order.

  


This occurs without either share ever leaving its owner or needing to know x or r. The mathematics makes unilateral signing structurally impossible: a valid on-chain signature requires both parties to have participated.

  


The Sovereign Share is kept on the user's device and never exposed to any third party. The Security Share lives in a Trusted Execution Environment (for privacy) or on-chain (for censorship-resistance) with a verifiable programmable policy engine. Neither share can sign a transaction alone. Policies can be set by the user, the dApp, or both to keep the user safe against known or evolving threats.

  


  


The policy engine enforces daily spend limits, whitelisted contracts, and auto-approve rules for routine operations. An agent literally cannot exceed what the owner defined. When a transaction falls outside that scope, a one-tap approve or deny goes to Telegram, email, SMS, or a hardware wallet. In this flow, agents propose a transaction, humans approve it, and on-chain protocols enforce its settlement.

  


WaaP also includes **Privileges** : scoped, time-bounded capabilities enforced server-side (maximum two hours) that let agents operate within pre-approved bounds without interrupting the human for every transaction. Every transaction is simulated before signing to catch malicious payloads before they execute.

  


WaaP supports all EVM chains at runtime, plus Sui and Stellar, with Solana coming in Q2 2026. WaaP wallets work as signers for ERC-4337 Smart Accounts, enabling full account abstraction including gas sponsorship, batched transactions, and session keys, on top of 2PC security across chains. The SDK is a drop-in EIP-1193 replacement compatible with wagmi, ethers.js, and viem. A headless CLI gives agents wallet access with the same policy enforcement applied. Integration is free.

  


## Passport: Who Is the Human Behind the Agent?

<https://docs.passport.xyz>

  


2PC solves what agents can do. Passport solves who authorized them to do it.

  


Human Passport is a ZK-powered proof-of-personhood system with over 2.4 million verified users, 43 million credentials issued, more than $512 million in capital protected from Sybil attacks, and 175+ partner projects. It is one of the most widely deployed proof-of-personhood systems in web3, originally built in Gitcoin to protect quadratic funding rounds and later folded into the human.tech stack by Holonym Foundation in December 2024.

  


Passport is how you prove a verified human exists at the root of every agent delegation chain. When an agent acts, the chain of authority traces back to a real person rather than a bot, a farmed wallet, or a Sybil. ZK credentials let users prove humanity, KYC status, and AML compliance on-chain without exposing raw identity data.

  


## Security Program: Audits, Adversarial Testing, Bug Bounties, and Open Research

  


WaaP's 2PC architecture has already been independently audited four times, by Cure53, Hexens, Least Authority, and Halborn, with additional security consulting from Anderson Software and Distrust. That is the baseline we are building from, not where we stop.

  


Funds from this round go toward expanding and publishing that work. We will run adversarial testing across agent wallet interaction patterns, Agent Privileges edge cases, and policy engine attack surfaces, specifically targeting the failure modes that appear when agents operate at scale.

  


We will commission independent audits of new architectural components as WaaP moves more components on-chain and as we ship ERC-8004-aligned agent identity support. We plan to open a bug bounty program to bring the broader Ethereum security community into the process.

  


Perhaps most importantly, we will publish. Agent wallet security is an unsolved problem at the infrastructure level, and the research should be in the commons. Threat models, audit findings, and architectural decisions will be published as open source work so the ecosystem can build on what we learn.
