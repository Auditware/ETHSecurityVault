## Description

**TID Research: Independent Risk Ratings to Protect Ethereum Users from the Next Hack**

It's 2026, and Ethereum users are getting drained at a pace the ecosystem hasn't seen since 2022. rsETH depegged after a contagion event ripped through Aave and Spark. Resolv's USR broke. Drift was exploited. WLFI left bad debt across Dolomite. Bridged DOT got siphoned. Every week another protocol, another asset, another set of users who didn't realize what they were holding until it was already too late.

The uncomfortable truth is that most of these hacks were _foreseeable_. The risk vectors — unaudited bridge contracts, concentrated admin keys, fragile collateral structures, oracle manipulation surfaces — were visible to anyone willing to do the work. But retail users don't have the time, the tooling, or the technical depth to do that work themselves. And the protocols that hold their funds have every incentive to downplay the risks rather than surface them.

That gap is what TID Research exists to close.

**What we do**

At TID Research (Today in DeFi), we publish independent risk ratings for DeFi assets and protocols on Ethereum and its L2s. Our goal is simple: give users the diligence layer they need to protect themselves before the next exploit hits, not after.

Our existing reports are live at <https://tidresearch.com/reports>, alongside dashboards at <https://tidresearch.com/dashboards/> that let users monitor exposures across the protocols they actually use.

For **assets** , our risk reports systematically cover:

  * **Smart contract risk**  — audit history, code maturity, upgradeability patterns, and the actual surface area an attacker would target. The kind of analysis that would have flagged rsETH's contract dependencies before the contagion.
  * **Bridge risk**  — for any wrapped or cross-chain asset, we trace the bridge architecture, validator set, and failure modes. Bridges remain one of the largest categories of value lost in Ethereum history.
  * **Admin key risk**  — who controls upgrades, who can pause, who holds the multisig keys, and what the real decentralization story is versus the marketing.
  * **Economic and collateral risk**  — the category that took down Terra UST, that's currently driving questions around MicroStrategy-backed instruments, and that will take down the next wave of yield-bearing assets if users don't understand what's actually backing them.



For **protocols** , our risk analysis covers smart contract attack surfaces, admin key control structures, oracle dependencies, and economic attack vectors like manipulation, governance capture, and liquidation cascades.

**Why this matters for Ethereum security**

When most people hear "Ethereum security" they think about audits, formal verification, and core protocol research. Those matter. But there's another layer of security that determines whether ordinary users actually keep their money: the layer of **information asymmetry between protocols and the people using them.**

A user can hold an asset that's economically broken without knowing it. A user can deposit into a vault whose admin key is held by a single EOA without knowing it. A user can stake into a protocol whose oracle can be manipulated for the cost of a flash loan without knowing it. Every one of those scenarios ends in the same place: users lose funds, trust in Ethereum erodes, and the ecosystem absorbs another reputational hit.

Independent, rigorous, free-to-read risk research is **public goods user protection infrastructure**. It's the layer that turns audit reports and on-chain data into something a regular user can actually act on. And it's exactly the kind of work that markets systematically underfund, because the people who need it most can't afford to pay for it, and the protocols being analyzed have an interest in _not_  paying for it.

That's why this round exists, and why we're applying.

**Why us**

TID Research has been operating in DeFi research and farming for years. We're not academics theorizing about risk — we're practitioners who've actively managed leveraged positions across Aave, Euler, Hyperlend, Velodrome, and others, watched liquidity crises play out in real time, and traded through multiple cycles of contagion. That hands-on experience is what lets us look at a protocol's docs and immediately spot the gap between what's promised and what's actually true on-chain.

We've covered breaking security stories as they happened, including the Drift exploit, the WLFI/Dolomite bad debt situation, rsETH contagion across lending markets, and the Aave/Spark 100% utilization spiral. Our subscriber base has come to rely on us for the kind of unvarnished, technically-grounded analysis that doesn't exist in mainstream crypto media.

We also publish openly. Our core risk reports are free. Our dashboards are free. The vast majority of what we produce is public goods — distributed widely so that the maximum number of users benefit, not gated behind paywalls only institutions can afford.

**What funding will support**

A grant from this round will directly fund expansion of our independent security and risk research:

  1. **More asset risk reports** , covering a wider range of LSTs, LRTs, stablecoins, yield-bearing tokens, and structured products on Ethereum mainnet and major L2s (Arbitrum, Base, Optimism, Hyperliquid, etc.).
  2. **Protocol-level risk reports** , going deeper than asset coverage into the lending markets, DEXes, perps platforms, and structured product venues where most user funds actually sit.
  3. **Faster post-incident analysis**  — when the next exploit happens, we want to publish technical breakdowns within hours, not days, so users on related protocols can act before contagion spreads.
  4. **Improved dashboards and monitoring** , so users can track the risk profile of their actual on-chain positions in real time, not just read static reports.
  5. **Open methodology**  — funding lets us document and open-source our risk frameworks so other researchers, DAOs, and risk teams can build on top of them.



**Conflicts and transparency**

We take editorial independence seriously. TID Research operates with disclosed conflicts of interest, and we're actively formalizing our entity structure, conflict-of-interest policies, and subscription terms modeled on the standards set by Blockworks Research and Delphi Digital. Any protocol we have a commercial or operational relationship with is disclosed in the relevant report. Funding from this round will be deployed exclusively toward independent research outputs published as public goods.
